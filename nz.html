Greeting Word: Kia Ora

Current Population: 5.1 Million

Location: About 4,606 miles (7,412 km) from Hawaii, forms the southwest apex of the Polynesian Triangle.

Size: covers approximately 103,000 square miles (268,000 km²) - about the size of Japan or the U.S. state of Colorado – and consists of two major islands as well as several smaller island groups.

Legend tells us of the first explorer-navigator-discoverer of the islands. According to legend, Maui and his brothers “fished up” the islands from the sea. The North Island is “The Fish of Maui” (a stingray). The South Island is his canoe. Stewart Island is the anchor stone.

Later, in the 10th century, Kupe & Ngake (Ngahue) ventured out southwest from Tahiti, following the traditional navigational instructions left by the hero Maui so many years earlier. The instructions were simple -- they were to keep their bearings towards a certain set of stars in the sky. In their canoe, carved by their own hands, they ventured out into the unknown and reached their destination. From far off, Kupe’s wife sighted land, and the gathering of white clouds around one of the mountain tops. She called out, “He ao! He ao!” (A cloud! A cloud!). Thus was Aotearoa discovered. It’s Maori name means, “Long White Cloud” and is commonly referred to as “The Land of the Long White Cloud”.

Polynesians have been known to set sail in search of new lands, their canoes were provisioned with food and plants for cultivation.

They remained isolated in New Zealand until the arrival of European explorers, the first of whom was the Dutchman Abel Tasman (1642). During that time, they grew in numbers to between 100,000 and 200,000, living almost exclusively on North Island. They adopted the name Maori to distinguish themselves from the Europeans, who after the voyages of the Englishmen Captain James Cook (1769-1777), began to come in greater frequency. Individuals, families, and extended family groups continue to acknowledge one another and are identified as descendants of specific tribal and sub-tribal ancestral leaders. These tribal leaders can trace their genealogy to the original settlers who arrived in multiple waves of migration aboard ocean-voyaging canoes.

It is situated in the South Pacific more than 1,000 miles southeast of Australia, its nearest neighbor. The country is comprised of two main islands – the North and South islands – and a number of small islands, some of them hundreds of miles from the main group. New Zealand is about 1,000 miles long and about 280 miles across at its widest point. The land area is approximately 103,000 square miles – slightly smaller than the state of Colorado and a little larger than the United Kingdom. The current population is 5.1 million, only about 17% of which are native Maori., making them the largest group of Polynesians in the Pacific today.

New Zealand’s climate differs from one part of the country to another. The larger South Island is colder than the North Island. Exceptionally bitter winds sweep the mountain summits of both islands and there is snow in the higher altitudes. Unlike other Polynesian islands, New Zealand experiences four distinct seasons.

Within one to two days drive, one can see glaciers, fiords, snow-capped mountains, vast plains and hills, sub- tropical forests, a volcanic plateau and white-sandy beaches. The spectacular scenery of both major islands has been featured in the Hollywood films, “Lord of the Rings“ & “Hobbit“ trilogies, “Wolverine“, “The Last Samurai“ and more. Aotearoa New Zealand is one of the youngest nations, with voyaging canoes bringing the first settlers from other Polynesian islands, as late as the 12th century.

MARAE: The Courtyard

The mare is defined as a courtyard – the open area in front of a Maori meeting house and also refers to the complex of other buildings surrounding it. Formal and informal interactions between people continue to take place in such settings. They are associated with tribes and families and are located in the historical tribal regions of these groups. All Maori connect to a marae through genealogy. It’s where their parents, grandparents, or ancestors are from. Through generations of intermarriage, Maori are able to connect to more than one marae.

The name of this particular marae is “Te Arohanui o Te Iwi Maori”, meaning, “The Great Love of the Maori People”. The name honors the Polynesian Cultural Center’s founding Maori performing arts group, “Te Arohanui Maori Company which came to Hawai’i in 1963 to assist in the final preparations of the Polynesian Cultural Center’s opening in October of that year.

PUHARA: Watchtower

The watchtower or Puhara, was built to protect the village. Puhara were manned 24-hours per day. A village was usually situated on top of a hill so that the extra height of the tower could allow a man to see for miles on all sides. If strangers were approaching, the man in the watchtower would blow a large wooden trumpet called the pukaea to warn the people in the village. A scout would then be sent to ascertain whether the strangers came in peace or in war. Maori protocol calls for a ceremony-of-sorts to be performed in these cases called powhiri in which the intent of the visitors is determined. You can see the powhiri protocol demonstrated here in this village.

PATAKA: Storage House

The food storage hut was also used to house heirlooms such as his jade pendants, weapons, adzes for canoe building, etc.

The Maori people ate fish, shellfish, birds, and small animals. They often salted, dried, or smoked various food items and placed them in gourds for storage. Sweet potatoes, tapioca, an d the 'Maori Potato' were food staples.

They also used roofed storage pits. These are partially underground with drainage on the sides of the house. They were not only used to store food, but also to store the plants which would be used next season.

As with other Polynesians, the Maoris cooked in open-air cooking spaces, in underground ovens called a hangi. A pit was dug and logs were placed inside with rocks on top of the logs. As the logs burned, the rocks fell into the pit. The food was then placed on the hot rocks and covered with leaves or mats to keep the heat in for several hours, steaming the food.

Other methods used were geothermal steaming, grilling food on sticks over embers, wrapping food in clay or leaves and placing them on the embers, boiling food by placing red-hot stones in wooden bowls of liquid.

WHARE TUPUNA:

Ancestral Meeting House

The Whare Tupuna, or the Ancestral Meeting House, is typical of those found in Maori villages in New Zealand. Such buildings are very important to the Maori people because this is where customs and ceremonies are maintained and perpetuated. In 1960, this particular building was commissioned, carved in New Zealand under the direction of Master Carver Hone Taiapa and shipped to Hawaii piece by piece where it was then put together for use here at the Polynesian Cultural Center.

This ancestral meeting house is named after Hawaikiroa, an ancient mariner who arrived in these islands (now known as Hawai’i) over 2,000 years ago, and according to legend, named them after himself.

The architecture and design of the building is full of symbolism. It represents the physical anatomy of the human body of the ancestor for whom the building is named. The apex of this building has two figures, the lower one, holding a paddle is Hawaikiroa, the common ancestor. The figure at the very top, represents his son. The facing boards on the gable of the house represent his outstretched arms, hands and fingers extended and reaching towards the ground. The ridgepole of the house represents his spine or backbone and the painted rafters, his rib cage.

Inside, the intricately carved figures on the walls represent the genealogy and family history of a tribe. Each figure or poupou depicts a specific ancestor and indicates information about him and his accomplishments. The eyes in the carvings were made from paua shell also known as abalone. The carvings depict tattooed faces, piercing eyes, protruding tongues and flexed leg muscles in dancing or fighting positions. These same traditional gestures are used today in dances performed in New Zealand and here at the Polynesian Cultural Center.

A person’s status in the community was determined by his bloodline. Hence, it was crucial for the Maoris to memorize and recite their genealogies. These carved figures are constant reminders to the people of their ancestry. The stories, histories and genealogies of these ancestors continues to be preserved in the carvings, the oral history and music of the people. The present generation continue to bear their names.

Painted scroll ornamentations on the rafters are more than just decorative. They’re called kowhaiwhai (circles within circles). They represent various foods and plants such as the sweet potato, peas, berries, and curling fern and are symbolic of nature and its importance. They tell stories of local history, symbolize aspects of the natural world and represent genealogies.

Between the carvings are the tukutuku panels that are woven by Maori women. Each design depicts a particular tribal historical legend that is passed from generation to generation through the weaving of these panels. The set of six panels on a stand at the end of the room tells the story of the original Maori migration to New Zealand. They are woven from the dried leaves of the kiekie vine, which is found abundantly throughout New Zealand. The yellow and white colors are the natural shades after drying has taken place.

KIA NGAWARI

Kia Ngāwari houses examples of the art of Māori weaving. Weaving techniques, include finger-weaving (related to twining), ornamental lattice work, patterned strand work, braiding, weft twining and plaiting

One of the more important varieties of native plant species found in Aotearoa New Zealand is the flax plant. The leaves can be used to craft mats, hats, bags, baskets, and even sails. The exposed inner fibers can be dyed and braided or woven into rope, fishing nets and the flax-made kilts used in the performing arts. Without the use of looms, weavers use the fibers to weave cloth, very similar to linen, which can be used as a wrap-around cloak. Many cloaks today are beautifully adorned with feathers.

Kupe

The exhibit inside of this carved meeting house, highlights the art of Maori carving.

  • Case #1 - Wood: Examples of long and short clubs made from native hardwood.

  • Case #2 - Stone: Examples of Nephrite Jade, Argillite, Garnet (used as a hammer stone), Obsidian and Sandstone.

  • Case #3 - Bone / Teeth / Shell: Examples of 8 shark species (found in Aotearoa New Zealand waters), abalone and whalebone.

  • Case #4 - Traditional Facial Markings: Examples of a bone chisel, mallet, ink bowl, tree resin, carved mask and portraits of male and female aristocracy. This practice became outlawed in 1907.

  • Case #5 - Dye: Examples of flax fibers, mordant (substance used to set dye on fibers and fabric) was made from the leaves of forest trees and shrubs, including the Celery Pine and Tea tree; forest tree bark; and iron-rich mud.

Photos on display include Hone Taiapa, MBE, the Master Carver; Matthew Cowley, the visionary of the PCC; numerous previous employees of the marae (now deceased) and the Te Arohanui Māori Company performers.

WAKA TAUA: War Canoe

This Waka Taua or war canoe of New Zealand is one of the greatest examples of Maori carving.

The canoe was named the ‘Long Fish of Māui’ (see Māui’s Legend above). Traditionally, a war canoe was used as a raiding vessel along the coastlines, inland lakes and rivers. Fully manned, it could reach a speed of up to 15 miles per hour (13 knots) for short distances. In some canoes, sails were also rigged to add to the speed.


HISTORY OF THE WAKA TAUA (WAR CANOE): TE IKA-ROA-A-MĀUI - The Long Fish of Māui

On the 6th of March 1948, it was announced that King George VI (r 1936-1952) and Queen Elizabeth would embark on a Royal Tour of Australia and New Zealand the following Spring of 1949, accompanied by their daughter Princess Margaret. They would be the first reigning monarchs to visit both countries.

As part of the many preparations that took place for this tour to New Zealand, a giant Kauri (Agathis Australis) tree from the Puketi State Forest, Northland was felled in October of 1948.

A Waka Taua (War Canoe) was to be crafted from this tree and used as part of the welcome ceremony for the Royal Family by the Ngāpuhi people. In addition, the canoe was to be presented as a gift to His Royal Highness.

On the 23rd of November 1948, the tour was cancelled due to the King’s declining health. However, the work of construction carried on until the hull of the canoe was sufficiently completed to be transported out of the bush. The canoe was brought to rest near the Kohewhata Marae Hall, Mangakahia Rd, Kaikohe.

The idea to complete the canoe resurfaced in the early 1960’s due to the Polynesian Village’s Project, which was already underway in Lā’ie, Hawai’i and Tuhikaramea, Aotearoa, NZ.

Members of the Witehira, Wihongi and Tahere families are pictured here donating the hull of the canoe to the Polynesian Villages Building Committee Supervisor, John Elkington, Sr. (front left) in la te 1962.



TOHUNGA WHAKAIRO (MASTER CARVERS)

Hone Taiapa was the Master Carver of the meeting house in Hawai’i, Hawaikiroa. He called on Piri Poutapu, also a Master Carver, to oversee the completion of the canoe. Two other experienced carvers joined the team to complete the canoe under his direction.

Tuti Tūkaokao carved the taurapa (stern post) and a majority of the rauawa (side boards). Jim Ruru carved the tauihu (prow-piece).

Hēmi Whautere Witehira (1881-1965) accompanied the canoe on its’ journey to Hawai’i in August of 1963. He is pictured here with Te Ika-roa-a-Māui canoe next to the lagoon at the Polynesian Villages which became known as the Polynesian Cultural Center.

TREE SPECIES:

KAURI

Agathis australis, commonly known by its Māori name Kauri, is a coniferous tree of the Araucariaceae in the genus of Agathis. Kauri trees can grow to over 50 metres (160+ feet) tall and up to 7 metres (23 feet) in diameter.

TŌTARA

Podocarpus totara (tōtara) is a species of podocarp tree. Tōtara trees can grow up to 40 metres (130 feet) tall and over 6 metres (16 feet) in diameter.

Te Ika-roa-ā-Māui is 18.2 metres (60 feet) and weighs close to 2500 kgs or 2 ½ tons. This canoe carries between 34 - 40 people inclusive of the paddlers, the steersman, the leader, and a bailer.

MAORI WARRIORS

This is a Taiaha. The Taiaha served as a broadsword quarterstaff, club, and spear. It is a fire-hardened wooden stave that had a long tapered, flattened shaft or blade, which broadens towards the end and has a head, nose, eyes, lips, teeth, and mouth, with a tongue-shaped spearhead on the opposite end.

The ancient Maori chiefs at ceremonial gatherings also used the taihaha. The Maori orator when making a speech emphasized his words with gestures and taiaha actions called taki.

The taiaha and greenstone mere (flat club) were both perfect weapons for close quarters combat. The advantage of a shorter club like the mere is its portability. It was usually tucked into the back waistband of the warrior’s skirt and hidden from sight behind a cloak.

Weapons were often named after an ancestor.

Activities

Tītī-tōrea

Traditionally, this game was one of many military games played in childhood. The objective of the game was to develop and maintain effective eye-hand coordination skills, essential in future hand-to- hand combat. The game still teaches those same skills today.

It requires players to simultaneously toss sticks back and forth to each other in various patterns and in rhythm to music or chant. Players try to catch the sticks without dropping any of them and to have fun.

Long-Poi

Poi is a light ball on a string of various lengths, swung or twirled rhythmically and in unison to sung accompaniment. Traditional poi can be made from bullrush (cat’s tail leaves) and attached to a flax cord or rope. Modern-day poi can be made with foam and plastic, while the handle can be plaited from yarn or cord.

Poi can be struck with either side of the hand and shoulder to create a percussive rhythm, imita te the sounds, movements and rhythms of nature. Matched with song, it can be used to enhance storytelling.

Terms:

Aotearoa – Māori name for New Zealand, meaning “Land of the Long White Cloud.”

Kia Ora – Common Māori greeting meaning “Hello” or “Be well.”

Māui – Legendary Polynesian hero who, according to myth, “fished up” the North and South Islands.

Kupe & Ngake (Ngahue) – Early Polynesian navigators from Tahiti credited with discovering Aotearoa around the 10th century.

He ao! He ao! – Phrase meaning “A cloud! A cloud!” exclaimed by Kupe’s wife upon sighting land.

Māori – Indigenous Polynesian people of New Zealand, descendants of early voyagers from other Pacific islands.

Marae – Courtyard and meeting area where formal and informal gatherings, ceremonies, and speeches take place.

Te Arohanui o Te Iwi Māori – Name of the Polynesian Cultural Center’s marae; means “The Great Love of the Māori People.”

Puhara – Watchtower built to guard the village; used to spot visitors or potential threats.

Pukaea – Large wooden trumpet blown from the watchtower to warn or welcome approaching visitors.

Powhiri – Ceremonial welcome that determines whether visiting groups come in peace or for conflict.

Pātaka – Elevated or roofed storage house used for food, weapons, and family treasures.

Hangi – Traditional underground oven where food is steamed over hot stones and covered with leaves.

Whare Tupuna – Ancestral meeting house representing the body of a revered ancestor; used for community gatherings.

Hawaikiroa – Name of the ancestral meeting house at the Polynesian Cultural Center, honoring an ancient mariner.

Kowhaiwhai – Painted scroll patterns on rafters symbolizing nature, food, and genealogy.

Tukutuku Panels – Woven wall panels made by Māori women that tell tribal legends and stories.

Kia Ngāwari – Exhibit showcasing Māori weaving techniques such as plaiting, braiding, and twining.

Harakeke (Flax) – Native plant used to make mats, baskets, ropes, cloaks, and other woven crafts.

Waka Taua – War canoe used for coastal raids and ceremonial events; elaborately carved and paddled by warriors.

Te Ika-roa-a-Māui – Name of the great war canoe meaning “The Long Fish of Māui.”

Kauri – Massive native conifer tree used to carve canoes; can grow over 50 meters tall.

Tōtara – Native podocarp tree often used for carving and building structures.

Tohunga Whakairo – Master carver skilled in traditional Māori wood carving.

Hone Taiapa & Piri Poutapu – Renowned master carvers who built the Hawaikiroa meeting house and war canoe.

Taiaha – Traditional wooden weapon combining spear and club features; used in battle and ceremonial speeches.

Mere (Pounamu Club) – Short jade weapon symbolizing status and skill in close combat.

Taki – Gesture movements performed during speeches or dances using the taiaha.

Tītī-tōrea – Stick-tossing game that trains rhythm, focus, and coordination; originally for warrior practice.

Poi – Ball on a string swung rhythmically in dance, symbolizing grace, rhythm, and storytelling.
