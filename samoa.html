Greeting Word: Talofa

Current Population: 260,000 (Samoa ~200,000; American Samoa ~60,000)

Location: About 2,600 miles (4,180 km) southwest of Hawaii

Size: Samoa – 1,130 sq mi (2,930 km²); American Samoa – 77 sq mi (199 km²)

Referred: The Heart of Polynesia


Because Samoa is in the center of the Polynesian triangle, it is referred to as “the Heart of Polynesia.” Geographically speaking, there are, in fact, two Samoas. Samoa is an independent country of about 1,130 square miles and includes the islands of Savai'i and Upolu. The other, American Samoa, is a territory of 77 square miles administered by the United States government and includes the eastern islands, primarily Tutuila. The population of Samoa is just under 200,000 and American Samoa has just under 60,000 people. Jacob Roggeveen, a Dutch Explorer, is credited with its discovery in 1722.

Although Samoa is two separate countries, its people, language and culture are one. From 1900 until the First World War, Western Samoa was a German territory. After World War I, it became a trust territory administered for the United Nations by New Zealand. In 1962, Western Samoa became the first independent Polynesian nation.

The constitution of American Samoa defines the basic governmental and legal organization. It provides for executive, legislative and judicial branches. The Governor and Lieutenant Governor head the executive branch and the bicameral legislature is popularly elected.

Samoans are raised in villages, or nu'u, such as this one at the Polynesian Cultural Center. The courtyard found in each village is called the malae and accommodates the traditional festivities and ceremonies of the village. Each of the structures in a Samoan village serves a special purpose.

Buildings in Samoa are usually rounded in shape with domed roofs. Posts are spaced evenly around each structure with large supporting beams in the center. The Samoans favor round shaped roofs not only for their beauty and symmetry, but also for practical purposes. The round shape allows the rain to quickly run off to the ground with out any obstructions. The dome shaped structure are also durable and up keep is minimal, but most importantly they keep the interior cool and breezy during hot, humid days. Samoa’s location near the equator means the weather is often hot and humid, which is why you will notice that none of the buildings have walls.


The breeze comes in off the ocean and helps to cool the occupants. Woven coconut leaf blinds, called pola, are attached to the beams of the buildings which can be lowered during strong winds, storms, or when privacy is desired. Roofing materials come from the leaves of the sugar cane plant, some of which are grown here in our plantation. The leaves are bundled together and cleverly attached in rows, round and around, until the whole roof is covered. A first time thatch job will last 10- 15 years without repairs – in the absence of excessively strong winds.

Tunoa or Umu Kuka: Kitchen

Samoan Kitchen and Cooking

In Samoa, the kitchen was the man’s domain because the preparation and cooking of food were considered physically demanding tasks.

The Samoan earth oven, called an umu, is a typical hot rock oven which is laid out above the ground.

Round river or lava rocks are used because they conduct heat extremely well.

Four logs are arranged in a square with rocks, and a fire is built to heat the rocks.

When the rocks are white with ash, charcoal debris is pushed aside and the food is placed on the heated rocks.

Fire-resistant leaves are used to protect the food so it is not burnt.

The whole oven is then covered with banana leaves and other insulating materials.

Roles and Respect in Samoan Culture

Hunting, climbing coconut trees, husking, cracking and milking coconuts all required physical strength.

The young men of the family show their respect to their parents, grandparents, and women by performing these arduous tasks.

The young men prepare the food and serve their grandparents and parents first.

Then the young children eat, and finally the cooks eat.

This service is part of the Samoan Way of Life, or Fa’a Samoa, in which respect is taught at a young age.

Young men serve their parents and elders in the same way their future children will serve them when they start their own families.

Importance of the Coconut

The coconut is one of the most important items in the life of Samoans and other Polynesians.

From the various parts of the coconut tree, materials for buildings, clothing, and food preparation are obtained.

The first step that must be done to use the coconut is to climb the very high tree to get it.

In the Samoan village, you can see how skillful the young men are at climbing the coconut trees, obtaining the coconuts, husking them and extracting its inner flesh for cooking purposes.

Coconut Processing

Once husked using a very sharp stick, the actual nut itself appears to have a face on one end.

Each coconut appears to have two eyes, a nose, and a mouth.

To crack open a coconut, a Samoan uses a heavy object or stone and hits the line that runs across the face.

The clear liquid inside the coconut is the juice.

Young coconuts that have not begun to produce much inner flesh contain more juice than ripe coconuts.

The coconut juice obtained from the very young coconuts is a refreshing drink in Samoa.

Making Coconut Milk

Inside the coconut is the white meat that can be grated and squeezed through a strainer made from various wood fibers.

The white liquid that falls from the grated coconut is coconut milk and is used in all types of Samoan cooking.

Other Samoan Foods

Other Samoan staple foods grown in the family garden include sugarcane, bananas, taro, tapioca, sweet potato, breadfruit, and the ava plant.

Meats include pork, chicken, fish, and shellfish of all kinds.

Village Life

Nu’u (Village): Traditional Samoan communities.

Malae: Central courtyard for festivities and ceremonies.

Buildings: Rounded with domed roofs. Open walls keep them cool and breezy.

Pola: Woven coconut blinds for storms, shade, and privacy.

Roofs: Made from sugarcane leaves, lasting 10–15 years.

Daily Life & Food

Tunoa / Umu Kuka (Kitchen): Earth oven with hot stones. Men cook as a sign of respect. Food is served in order: elders, children, then cooks.

Coconuts: Used for food, drink, housing, and clothing. Coconut milk comes from grated coconut flesh.

Staple Foods: Sugarcane, bananas, taro, tapioca, sweet potato, breadfruit, and ava.

Meats: Pork, chicken, fish, and shellfish.

Traditional Houses

Fale Nonofo (Family House)

Used for sleeping, eating, and crafts. Mats are used for bedding and rolled up daily.

Fale Talimalo (Guest House) & Fale Fono (Meeting House)

Hosts guests and village meetings. Chiefs gather here to discuss community matters. Special posts indicate rank. Also home of the Kava Ceremony.

Maota o le Ali’i / Maota Tofa (Chief’s House)

Elevated and prestigious. Ali’i (High Chiefs) make decisions, Tulafale (Talking Chiefs) speak for them. Furnished with mats (traditional wealth). Chiefs use symbolic items like:

  • To’oto’o (Staff): Authority to speak
  • Fue (Swish): Power of speech
  • Tanoa fai ava (Ava Bowl): Peace, unity, and brotherhood
  • Tao (Spear): Warrior strength and protection

Tatau: The Art of Tattooing

Practiced for over 2,000 years. Introduced by Fijian women Taema and Tifaiga.

Traditionally compulsory for men between 14–18. Tattoos extend from hips to knees.

Done with a comb-like instrument tapped with a mallet. Painful but symbolizes adulthood, rank, cultural pride, and beauty.

Women were tattooed as well, but less extensively.
